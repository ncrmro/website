I've been building a reentry trajectory simulator and validating it
against historical missions: start the simulation from the exact point
in space where a real capsule hit the atmosphere, propagate it down,
and measure how far the simulated landing point is from where the
recovery crew actually found the vehicle. Apollo 11 is the gold-standard
test case — fully declassified, exhaustively documented, and a *lifting*
entry, meaning the capsule steered itself by rolling left and right
during its nine minutes of descent.

That steering is the problem. If you simulate Apollo 11 with a
closed-loop autopilot that aims for the known splashdown point, you get
a flattering number — my simulator gets within 17.6 km that way. But
that's the answer key grading itself: a good controller will hide a
mediocre physics model by continuously correcting toward the target.
The honest test is to replay the **exact roll commands the real
spacecraft executed, at the same times, open loop**, and let the physics
land wherever it lands.

And the record of those commands exists! NASA published it in 1970, in
R. H. Manders' *Apollo 11 Entry Postflight Analysis*. There's just one
catch: it exists as **Figure 9** — a hand-drawn curve on graph paper,
scanned at an angle, with annotation arrows and typewriter labels
overlapping the data.

![Page 29 of the Manders 1970 report: the figure is rotated sideways on the page, hand-drawn, with binder holes punched through the margin.](/posts/extracting-ground-truth-from-1970-apollo-scans/media/manders-fig9-scan.png)

No table. No appendix. Fifty-five years later, if you want the numbers,
someone has to get them off this picture.

## The options that didn't fit

**WebPlotDigitizer** is the standard answer, and it's a good tool: load
the image, click known axis points to calibrate, click along the curve,
export CSV. For one figure it's maybe fifteen minutes of careful
clicking. But this figure is one of *many* — the same report has five
more charts I need (altitude, velocity, load factor histories), and the
companion aerodynamics report has another three. Worse, manual clicking
produces a CSV with no machine-checkable provenance. I wanted an
extraction that a reviewer could re-run from the archived PDF and get
the same numbers.

**Engauge Digitizer** is in nixpkgs and has semi-automatic curve
tracing, which sounded promising — but it's built around its GUI, and
the automatic mode assumes a clean plot. Which brings us to why
"automatic" tools generally fail here:

A 1970 NASA chart is *hostile* to naive curve extraction. The data
curve, the gridlines, the annotation leader lines ("ENTER FINAL PHASE
→"), and the typewritten labels are all the same black ink. A
column-by-column "follow the dark pixels" tracer will happily walk off
the data and down an annotation arrow, and you won't know unless
something *looks* at the result.

That last clause turned out to be the whole answer.

## An agent with eyes on its own output

I pointed Claude Fable (via Claude Code) at the problem. What made it
work wasn't that the model knew some magic computer-vision incantation —
the tracer it wrote is honestly simple. It's that the agent could run a
**closed verification loop on its own work**: write the tracer, render
the extracted points as red dots *on top of the original scan*, look at
the image, diagnose the failure, patch the tracer, repeat.

The pipeline it converged on:

1. Render the PDF page at 600 dpi and rotate it to landscape.
2. **Self-calibrate the axes** by finding the gridlines: sum the ink in
   each pixel row and column inside the plot box — gridlines show up as
   spikes. Fitting all 13 vertical and 19 horizontal lines pinned the
   mapping from pixels to seconds and degrees with residuals under two
   pixels, about a quarter second and a quarter degree. No human
   clicking on axis points.
3. Mask the gridlines out, then trace the curve column by column,
   linking each column's ink to the previous one by continuity.

The first run produced this:

![Zoomed crop of the scan with the traced points in red: the trace follows the curve's plateau, then slides off down the "ENTER FINAL PHASE" annotation leader line and dies in the label text.](/posts/extracting-ground-truth-from-1970-apollo-scans/media/failure-leader-line.png)

The red trace rides the curve beautifully — then slides off down the
annotation leader line and ends up tracing the words "ENTER FINAL
PHASE". This is exactly the failure mode that makes people give up on
automatic digitization. The agent looked at the overlay, named the
problem, and added a prior: the hand-drawn data curve is about three
times thicker than the ruled leader lines, so prefer thick ink runs.

That fixed the label dive but not the junction itself, where the leader
line *touches* the curve and is drawn with the same pen. Slope-memory
tracking didn't save it either, because the trace approaches the
junction on a downhill wiggle and happily continues downhill onto the
leader. The fix was almost cheeky: **trace that span of the chart
backwards.** Approached from the right, the curve is flat through the
junction and the leader line departs at an angle, so continuity keeps
you on the data.

![The same junction after the fix: red points ride the flat through the leader junction, up past the inflight-measured-data circles, over the +100 degree crest and down into the dip — never touching the annotation.](/posts/extracting-ground-truth-from-1970-apollo-scans/media/reverse-trace-junction.png)

One region resisted automation entirely — and it's the most dramatic
part of the flight. Around 90 seconds in, Apollo rolls hard from lift-up
to full lift-down: the command dives to −180°, rails along the bottom
edge of the plot (where it's indistinguishable from the axis line the
tracer must mask), pops back up, rails again, and spikes through the
"enter upcontrol" maneuver:

![High-zoom crop of the bank-reversal region: near-vertical strokes diving to the bottom border of the plot, a narrow spike, and a long straight climb back up.](/posts/extracting-ground-truth-from-1970-apollo-scans/media/bank-reversal-rails.png)

For those 47 seconds the agent did what a human with WebPlotDigitizer
would do — zoomed in and read the chart — except its "clicks" were
22 hand-transcribed points written into the assembly script with a
comment documenting the method and the ±3° accuracy estimate. Everything
else, 484 of the 506 points, is the tracer's.

![The full figure with the final trace overlaid: red dots track the entire roll-command history across all nine minutes.](/posts/extracting-ground-truth-from-1970-apollo-scans/media/final-overlay.png)

The committed artifacts are the point-by-point CSV, the tracer script,
and a sidecar note with the calibration residuals — so the extraction is
reproducible from the archived PDF by machine, which was the property
manual clicking couldn't give me.

## Did we get it right? The flight itself says yes

Here's my favorite part. You can validate a digitization *with physics*.

I fed the extracted roll history into the simulator as an open-loop
replay — fly exactly what Apollo flew, no corrections — and decomposed
the landing miss into two directions: along the flight path, and
sideways across it.

The result: **867 km of error along-track, 14 km cross-track.**

Those two numbers mean completely different things. Apollo's roll
commands alternate left and right precisely so that the sideways
drift cancels out — that's how the real guidance managed cross-range.
If I had digitized the curve wrong, gotten a sign convention backwards,
or misread the bank reversals, the cancellation would break and the
cross-track error would be enormous. Fourteen kilometers over a
2,500 km flight is the cancellation *working*. The chart was read
correctly.

The along-track error is something else entirely: it's an honest
measurement of my simulator's aerodynamics model, which currently uses
a crude linear reconstruction of the Apollo capsule's lift and drag
(the *real* curves are figure-only in another 1969 report — Hillje's
entry aerodynamics paper — which is next in line for the same tracer).
Open-loop replay is unforgiving by design: with no controller papering
over model error, an imperfect drag schedule means the replayed bank
maneuvers execute at the wrong speeds and altitudes, and the energy
bookkeeping drifts hundreds of kilometers. That 50× gap between the
closed-loop number (17.6 km) and the open-loop number (872 km) is the
cleanest demonstration I've seen of why "my simulation hits the
target" is not validation when the simulation is steering toward the
target.

## The takeaway

The thing that made this tractable wasn't a smarter algorithm — column
tracing with an ink-thickness prior and slope memory is something any
of us could have written in 2010. It's that each failure mode was
*visible* but not *predictable*: nobody writes "prefer thick runs" or
"trace this span right-to-left" into version one. Those patches came
from an agent rendering its own output onto the source scan, looking at
it the way a person would, and iterating — three rounds, a few minutes
each. Vision in the loop turns plot digitization from a manual chore
into a code-review problem.

Somewhere in NASA's archives there are thousands more figures like this
one — wind-tunnel curves, trajectory reconstructions, telemetry plots —
each one the only surviving record of measurements that cost millions
of 1960s dollars to make. The data was never lost. It's just been
waiting for readers with patience, and patience is exactly what
machines are good at.
